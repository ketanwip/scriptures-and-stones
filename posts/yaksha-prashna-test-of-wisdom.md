The Pandavas had been walking for three days through the Kamyaka forest, their feet cracked and swollen, when Yudhishthira noticed something wrong with the wind. It no longer carried the scent of sal and mahua blossoms. Instead, there was an absence—a complete olfactory void that any trained kshatriya would recognize as **supernatural interference**.

Nakula, the youngest, collapsed first. His body's core temperature had dropped to dangerous levels, the symptoms identical to what the Ayurvedic texts call *Vata-Pitta imbalance*—but accelerated, as if years of deterioration had been compressed into moments. Within minutes, Sahadeva followed, then Arjuna, then the mighty Bhima. All four lay motionless on the forest floor, their pupils dilated, respiratory rates approaching zero.

Yudhishthira stood alone among his brothers' bodies. He had seen death before—on battlefields, in dice halls—but this was different. There were no wounds, no visible trauma. Just the eerie stillness of men who had been fighting and breathing moments ago, now frozen like marble statues.

He followed their trail backward and found what he was looking for: a lake. The water was impossibly clear, its surface reflecting the canopy above with such precision that it seemed less like a body of water and more like a *portal*—a threshold between the material and the metaphysical. The Pandavas had drunk from it. That much was obvious from the wet earth around the shore and the discarded *kamandalu* (water vessel) lying nearby.

"You did not heed my warning."

The voice came from everywhere and nowhere—a phenomenon the Upanishads describe as *Shabda Brahman*, sound as the fundamental vibration of existence itself. Yudhishthira's hand moved instinctively to where his sword would be, but he had left it with the camp supplies.

From the center of the lake, a figure rose. Not from *beneath* the water, but from *within* it, as if the lake itself were reorganizing its molecular structure to assume human form. The being stood nearly twelve feet tall, its body composed of what appeared to be condensed vapor—neither solid nor liquid, but something in between. The texts would later identify this as a **Yaksha**, a nature spirit of immense power, guardian of hidden treasures and keeper of cosmic law.

"Your brothers," the Yaksha said, "ignored my condition. They drank without permission. They took without asking. And so they have paid the price that all thieves pay."

Yudhishthira studied the creature. In the lore of the Puranas, Yakshas were described as *dhanādhipati*—lords of wealth—but also as agents of *dharma*, cosmic order. They could be benevolent or brutal, depending on whether one approached them with respect or arrogance. His brothers had been dying of thirst; they would have seen water and taken it without thought. It was a understandable mistake, but in the presence of divine law, understandable was not the same as forgivable.

"What must I do," Yudhishthira asked, his voice steady despite the horror of the situation, "to restore them?"

"Answer my questions," the Yaksha replied. "Answer correctly, and you may choose one brother to revive. Answer incorrectly, and you will join them in *Yama-loka*, the realm of death."

The negotiation was already tilted. Four lives for one. But Yudhishthira recognized the underlying architecture of the test—this was not about the answers themselves, but about the *quality of reasoning* behind them. The Yaksha was measuring something deeper than knowledge. He was measuring *wisdom*.

"Begin," Yudhishthira said.

The questions came like arrows, each one designed to pierce through layers of conventional thinking:

*"What makes the Sun rise?"*  
"**Brahma** makes the Sun rise."

*"What is swifter than wind?"*  
"**Mind** is swifter than wind."

*"What is the one thing that, once renounced, makes one wealthy?"*  
"**Desire**—renouncing desire makes one wealthy."

*"What enemy is unconquerable?"*  
"**Anger** is the unconquerable enemy."

Each answer came without hesitation, pulled from the deep well of Yudhishthira's study under the sages Kripa and Drona, but also from lived experience—thirteen years of forest exile, humiliation, loss. The questions accelerated, probing deeper:

*"What is the most wonderful thing in this world?"*

Yudhishthira paused. This was the fulcrum, the pivot point. The Yaksha was not asking for cleverness now; he was asking for truth.

"Day after day," Yudhishthira said slowly, "countless creatures meet their death, yet those who remain believe themselves to be immortal. **This is the most wonderful thing**."

For the first time, the Yaksha's form rippled—a sign of acknowledgment, perhaps even approval.

The final question came with the weight of inevitability: "You may revive **one** brother. Choose."

Yudhishthira looked at the bodies of his brothers. Arjuna, the greatest archer in the world, the one who could win wars. Bhima, whose strength could tear down fortress walls. Nakula and Sahadeva, the twins, sons of Madri.

The strategic choice was obvious: Arjuna or Bhima. Either one could protect the others, could ensure survival in the forest, could eventually reclaim their kingdom.

But Yudhishthira said: "**Nakula**."

The Yaksha tilted his massive head. "Why not Arjuna? Why not Bhima? They are sons of Kunti, your mother. The twins are sons of Madri, your father's second wife. Why choose outside your own blood?"

"Because," Yudhishthira replied, "my father Pandu had two wives. If I live—Kunti's son lives. If Nakula or Sahadeva live—Madri's son lives. **Both mothers should have a surviving child**. This is *dharma*."

The lake exploded with light.

When Yudhishthira's vision cleared, all four of his brothers were breathing again, their chest rising and falling with the steady rhythm of restored life. The Yaksha's form had changed—no longer vapor, but solid, radiant, crowned.

"You have recognized me," the figure said, and Yudhishthira understood. This was no Yaksha. This was **Yama-Dharmaraja**, the Lord of Death himself, who was also—by the secret blessing of a curse—Yudhishthira's true father.

"I came to test you," Yama said, "before the final war. I needed to know if the son I planted in mortal form would choose dharma over blood, principle over power. You have answered well."

Then the God of Death did something gods rarely do: he bowed.

"When the great war comes," Yama said, "and all the rules are broken, **remember this moment**. Remember that dharma is not what wins battles—it is what remains when all the battles are over."

And with that, he vanished, leaving only clear water, five brothers, and the scent of mahua blossoms returning to the wind.
